For the academics in the room

How industry hopes you'll learn to use AI

A quick detour into how AI-first companies are starting to work — and what they'll want from the scientists they hire.

The shift industry is betting on

  • Humans amplified by AI — "AI-first is not AI-only." the valued move is orchestrating AI, not doing every step by hand
  • Teams organize around outcomes, not job titles — people go more full-stack and let AI fill the skill gaps.
  • The new bottleneck isn't building — it's iterating: prototype, ship, learn, repeat.

Framing borrowed from NFX, "AI-First Company Structure: Mission Pods." DISCLAIMER: NfX is one of my investors so I kind of have to listen to them :p

LLMs still need your specific expertise

  • Without it, you just pile up half-finished, never-shipped work — you feel fast, but you're spinning your wheels.
  • So the scarce skill becomes quality control: evaluating what the AI actually produced. Industry calls it eval culture.
  • Things like ELN reviews is eval culture for your own science — which is exactly what we'll practice next.

ELN review is just one example. I'm positive you all have some tedious, time-consuming task that you hate doing but needs to get done!

Using AI to automate the low-reward but high-repetition tasks of science.

Nobody lists documentation as their favorite part of science, but everyone's work depends on it being done correctly and consistently. See: Lesson 3.

A review is only as good as what you make Claude check against.

And what to check is a thing only you know. The point of the hour isn't to learn my personal ELN checklist, it's to teach Claude yours, and to see how you can use Claude to automate beyond data analyses.

Step one

Let Claude interview you

Pull your own review checklist out of your head, one question at a time.

Let Claude interview you

  • Don't explain everything up front, have Claude ask you. "Interview me, one question at a time, about what I'd check in an entry from my own work."
  • Answer honestly and specifically. This is the real work of the hour!
  • Example: recompute the dilution math; did the concentrations actually come out to what the entry claims?

Nudge if it asks in prose: "ask me with the question buttons, a few options each, one at a time."

Step two

Turn your answers into a skill

Flush it into your ClaudeLab so you never have to re-explain it.

Build the /review-entry skill

  • Save your checklist as a reusable skill in your ClaudeLab. "Save that as a skill called review-entry so I can run /review-entry on any entry."
  • All you have to do is explain. Claude writes the skill; you brought the expertise.

Step three

Run it on a real entry

Point your skill at a notebook entry that initially looks complete, but has some sneaky errors.

Run it on a real entry

  • Point /review-entry at the batch-3 analysis entry (a Word doc). notebook/2025-07-16_analysis-entry-for-review.docx
  • An ELN entry is evidentiary material, much of what needs checking lives in what it links to: systems of record, metadata annotations, protocol deviations, etc. tell the skill to follow links, open the analysis, and recompute the numbers; trust the write-up but verify!
  • It'll catch some things and miss others because it only knows what you've told it. The "institutional knowledge" in your head needs to get dumped out explicitly.

When it misses something, teach it

  • Find a problem your skill didn't flag. there's a subtle one in the batch-4 injection-prep entry... ask where a number actually came from
  • Close the loop: "You missed this. Add a check for it so you catch it every time."
  • Your Claude auto-reviewer just learned something permanent, a living SOP, for you and anyone you share it with.

What you take with you

  • A good review is personal and will depend on your own specific workflows. The mistakes worth catching are the ones from your bench and your pipeline.
  • Trust your scientists/trainees/self, but don't trust the write-up! Perform the review, then review the review.
  • Your skill is a living SOP, so every miss becomes a new check, caught forever after.
